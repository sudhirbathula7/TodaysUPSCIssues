ISSUE TITLE:
Strengthening Urban Flood Governance

GS PAPER:
GS III

SUBJECT:
Disaster Management | Urban Governance

RATING:
4.8/5

RECALL QUESTION 1:
Why are Indian cities increasingly vulnerable to urban flooding?

RECALL QUESTION 2:
How can urban flood governance be improved?

CURRENT CONTEXT:
Rapid urbanisation, loss of wetlands, concretisation of drainage channels and inadequate storm-water infrastructure have increased flooding in Indian cities. Extreme rainfall now overwhelms drainage systems, exposing weaknesses in urban planning and disaster preparedness.

WHY IT MATTERS FOR UPSC:
Urban flooding links disaster management, climate adaptation, municipal governance and environmental conservation. UPSC frequently examines institutional failures, ecological degradation and practical solutions involving urban local bodies, planning authorities and disaster-management agencies.

CORE CONCEPT:
Urban flooding occurs when rainfall exceeds a city's drainage capacity. Unlike river floods, it is largely driven by blocked drains, wetland loss, unplanned construction, land-use changes and inadequate infrastructure maintenance.

CHALLENGES:
Major challenges include fragmented institutions, outdated drainage maps, encroached water bodies, poor solid-waste management, weak enforcement of building regulations, inadequate forecasting and poor coordination among urban agencies.

WAY FORWARD:
Cities should restore wetlands, modernise drainage plans, strengthen early-warning systems, enforce climate-resilient building norms and improve municipal capacity. Community participation is essential for protecting drains and local water bodies.

QUICK FACTS:
Urban flooding is usually localised and develops quickly. Wetlands act as natural water-storage systems. Storm-water drains must remain separate from sewage networks. Urban local bodies are primarily responsible for drainage, sanitation and local land-use management.

WHAT UPSC ASKS:
UPSC may ask about causes of urban floods, institutional weaknesses, climate-resilient urban planning, the role of urban local bodies and the importance of wetlands and drainage systems.

KEY TAKEAWAY:
Urban flooding is not merely a rainfall problem; it reflects failures in planning, drainage, ecological protection and municipal governance.

Improving Parliamentary Committee Scrutiny

GS PAPER:
GS II

SUBJECT:
Polity | Parliament | Governance

RATING:
4.7/5

RECALL QUESTION 1:
Why are parliamentary committees important for legislative scrutiny?

RECALL QUESTION 2:
What reforms can improve the functioning of parliamentary committees?

CURRENT CONTEXT:
The increasing passage of Bills with limited debate has renewed attention on parliamentary committees. These committees allow detailed examination of legislation, expenditure and government performance away from political confrontation on the floor of Parliament.

WHY IT MATTERS FOR UPSC:
Parliamentary committees are central to legislative accountability, executive oversight and informed lawmaking. UPSC may ask about their functions, limitations, constitutional position and contribution to deliberative democracy.

CORE CONCEPT:
Parliamentary committees are smaller groups of Members of Parliament constituted to examine Bills, budgets, policies and government departments. They enable detailed discussion, expert consultation and cross-party scrutiny that is difficult during regular parliamentary proceedings.

CHALLENGES:
Committee recommendations are generally advisory, many Bills are not referred to committees, meetings lack transparency, research support is limited and frequent political disruptions reduce parliamentary deliberation. Short tenures may also weaken institutional expertise.

WAY FORWARD:
Important Bills should normally undergo committee scrutiny. Committees need stronger research assistance, sufficient time, regular attendance and systematic follow-up on recommendations. Reports should be made accessible while protecting confidential deliberations where necessary.

QUICK FACTS:
Department-related Standing Committees examine ministries and Demands for Grants.
Select Committees examine specific Bills before they are passed.
The Public Accounts Committee examines CAG reports on government expenditure.
Committee recommendations are advisory but improve legislative scrutiny and accountability.

WHAT UPSC ASKS:
UPSC may examine the role of committees in executive accountability, legislative quality, financial oversight and strengthening parliamentary democracy.

KEY TAKEAWAY:
Effective parliamentary committees improve lawmaking by combining detailed scrutiny, expert evidence and bipartisan deliberation.

ISSUE TITLE:
Building Resilient Public Health Systems

GS PAPER:
GS II

SUBJECT:
Health | Governance | Social Justice

RATING:
4.8/5

RECALL QUESTION 1:
What makes a public health system resilient?

RECALL QUESTION 2:
How can India strengthen preparedness for future health emergencies?

CURRENT CONTEXT:
Recent disease outbreaks have shown that health systems require more than hospitals and treatment facilities. Surveillance, primary healthcare, trained personnel, laboratories, reliable data and coordination between different levels of government are essential for managing health emergencies.

WHY IT MATTERS FOR UPSC:
Public health resilience relates to welfare governance, federalism, human resources, health financing and emergency preparedness. UPSC may ask how India can move from reactive disease control towards prevention, early detection and coordinated response.

CORE CONCEPT:
A resilient health system can prepare for, absorb, respond to and recover from health shocks while continuing essential services. It depends on preventive care, disease surveillance, primary health institutions, trained workers, supply chains and public trust.

CHALLENGES:
India faces uneven health infrastructure, shortages of doctors and nurses, weak rural facilities, fragmented disease data, low public spending, supply-chain gaps and coordination difficulties among Union, State and local authorities.

WAY FORWARD:
India should strengthen primary healthcare, expand laboratory networks, improve integrated disease surveillance, maintain emergency reserves, train health workers and promote cooperation among governments. Transparent communication and community participation are equally important for maintaining public trust.

QUICK FACTS:
Public health focuses on population-level prevention and protection. Primary healthcare is the first point of contact. Disease surveillance supports early warning. Health is primarily a State subject, while the Union also supports national programmes and coordination.

WHAT UPSC ASKS:
UPSC may ask about public health infrastructure, preventive healthcare, health federalism, disease surveillance, primary healthcare and lessons from epidemics.

KEY TAKEAWAY:
Public health resilience requires prevention, surveillance, primary care, trained personnel and coordinated governance before a crisis begins.

ISSUE TITLE:
Protecting India’s Coastal Ecosystems

GS PAPER:
GS III

SUBJECT:
Environment | Climate Change | Geography

RATING:
4.6/5

RECALL QUESTION 1:
Why are coastal ecosystems important for climate resilience?

RECALL QUESTION 2:
What measures can reduce pressure on India’s coastal regions?

CURRENT CONTEXT:
India’s coastal regions face erosion, habitat destruction, pollution, unplanned construction, rising sea levels and stronger cyclonic events. Mangroves, wetlands, sand dunes and coral ecosystems are increasingly important for reducing disaster risks and supporting livelihoods.

WHY IT MATTERS FOR UPSC:
Coastal ecosystems connect environmental conservation, disaster management, climate change, fisheries, livelihoods and sustainable development. UPSC may test both ecological significance and governance mechanisms for coastal regulation.

CORE CONCEPT:
Coastal ecosystems provide natural protection against storms and erosion, store carbon, support fisheries and maintain biodiversity. Their destruction increases the vulnerability of settlements and infrastructure to climate-related hazards.

CHALLENGES:
Major challenges include illegal construction, industrial pollution, tourism pressure, sand mining, weak enforcement of coastal rules, livelihood conflicts and inadequate coordination among environmental, fisheries, tourism and urban-development authorities.

WAY FORWARD:
India should restore mangroves and dunes, enforce coastal regulations, control pollution, map vulnerable zones and involve fishing communities in conservation. Infrastructure planning must account for sea-level rise, erosion patterns and ecosystem services.

QUICK FACTS:
Mangroves reduce wave energy and coastal erosion. Coral reefs support marine biodiversity. Coastal Regulation Zone rules regulate activities near the coast. Coastal communities depend heavily on fisheries and ecosystem health.

WHAT UPSC ASKS:
UPSC may ask about mangroves, coastal erosion, coastal regulation, blue economy, climate adaptation and community-based conservation.

KEY TAKEAWAY:
Healthy coastal ecosystems are essential natural infrastructure for biodiversity, livelihoods and protection against climate-related disasters.
